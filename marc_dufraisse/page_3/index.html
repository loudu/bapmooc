HALL OF FAMES

The history of computers has introduced five different generations of computing devices. Each of these generations is characterized by a major technological development changing the way computers operate, resulting in increasingly smaller, cheaper, more powerful, efficient and reliable computers.

A selection of famous computers

The first generation (1940-1956) used vacuum tubes for circuitry and magnetic drums for memory, and were often enormous, taking up entire rooms.





The second generation (1956-1963) used transistors instead of vacuum tubes, allowing computers to become smaller, faster, cheaper, more energy-efficient and reliable. 

The third generation (1964-1971) used silicon chips with a large number of transistors, which drastically increased the speed and efficiency of computers. The fourth generation (1971-present) uses VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration) microprocessors, allowing a new giant step. The ENIAC, 80 by 3 foot in 1945, was shrinked in 1995 to a 8 by 8 mm silicon chip containing 250,000 transistors running at 20 MHz. By comparison, Intel's Core i7 microprocessor integrates 781 million transistors at 3.60 GHz.



The fifth generation (present and beyond) will be based on massive parallelism, internet and artificial intelligence (cf. chapter 7).
